01
Tree of Life
Hebrew: עֵץ הַחַיִּים (Etz HaChayyim) · Greek: ξύλον τῆς ζωῆς
From Eden's center to the New Jerusalem — the Bible's most enduring symbol of eternal life in God's presence. What was barred in Genesis 3 is fully restored in Revelation 22.
Genesis 2:9 WOL ↗
Jehovah God caused to grow from the ground every tree that was pleasing to look at and good for food. He also placed the tree of life in the middle of the garden and the tree of the knowledge of good and bad.
The Tree of Life stands at the very center of Eden, signaling that access to God's eternal life is the heart of paradise. Ancient Near Eastern cultures often depicted a "cosmic tree" at the world's center; Scripture redeems this motif, placing God himself — not mythology — as the source of life.
Genesis 3:22–24 WOL ↗
Jehovah God said: "Here the man has become like one of us in knowing good and bad. Now we must not let him put his hand out and take fruit also from the tree of life and eat and live forever."
God's restriction of the Tree of Life after the Fall is an act of mercy, not cruelty. Immortality without holiness would be a permanent curse. Cherubim — divine guardians also found on the Ark of the Covenant — stand watch. The eastern gate becomes a recurring motif pointing toward ultimate restoration.
Proverbs 3:18 WOL ↗
It is a tree of life to those who take hold of it, and those who keep firm hold of it will be called happy.
Divine Wisdom is here personified as the Tree of Life — transforming it from a geographical place into a spiritual reality accessible to the faithful in every generation. To pursue wisdom is to partake of life itself.
Revelation 22:2 WOL ↗
On both sides of the river were trees of life producing 12 crops of fruit, yielding their fruit each month. And the leaves of the trees were for the healing of the nations.
In the New Jerusalem, the Tree of Life is not merely restored — it is glorified. Bearing fruit every month, its leaves heal the nations. What was restricted to two people in a garden is now open to all humanity. This is the ultimate arc of redemption: from barred gate to healing leaves for every nation.

03
Fig Tree
Hebrew: תְּאֵנָה (Te'enah) · Greek: συκῆ (sykē)
Mentioned in over 50 biblical texts — from the leaves that covered Adam and Eve to the fig tree cursed by Jesus. The fig is Scripture's most versatile symbol: of shame, peace, prosperity, and divine judgment.
Genesis 3:7 WOL ↗
Then the eyes of both of them were opened, and they realized they were naked. So they sewed fig leaves together and made loin coverings for themselves.
The fig tree's very first appearance is linked to human shame and humanity's first attempt at self-made salvation. Fig leaves — large enough to seem sufficient — prove inadequate. God later replaces them with animal skins, inaugurating Scripture's great theme: human covering versus God's provision.
1 Kings 4:25 WOL ↗
And Judah and Israel continued to dwell in security, everyone under his own vine and his own fig tree, from Dan to Beer-sheba, all the days of Solomon.
Sitting under one's vine and fig tree becomes the Bible's defining image of shalom — peace, prosperity, and security. This Solomonic golden age sets the standard against which all subsequent eras are measured, and its echo resounds in the prophetic visions of Micah 4:4 and Zechariah 3:10.
Matthew 21:18–19 WOL ↗
While returning to the city early in the morning, he felt hungry. He caught sight of a fig tree by the road and went to it, but he found nothing on it except leaves, and he said to it: "Let no fruit come from you ever again." And the fig tree withered instantly.
The only miracle of destruction in the Gospels — and a deeply prophetic act. Framed around the cleansing of the Temple, it symbolizes religious formalism: impressive in appearance (leaves, a bustling Temple) but bearing no spiritual fruit. The WOL library has extensive commentary explaining why Jesus acted even though it was not the season for figs.
John 1:48 WOL ↗
Nathanael said to him: "How do you know me?" Jesus answered him: "Before Philip called you, while you were under the fig tree, I saw you."
Jesus' knowledge of Nathanael's private moment under the fig tree triggers one of the New Testament's most immediate confessions of faith: "You are the Son of God; you are the King of Israel!" The fig tree — a quiet, contemplative spot — becomes the hinge of Nathanael's encounter with the divine. God sees us even when unseen.

04
Olive Tree
Hebrew: זַיִת (Zayit) · Greek: ἐλαία (elaia)
From Noah's dove to Paul's grafting allegory in Romans 11 — the olive tree may be the most theologically rich tree in all of Scripture. Its oil fueled the Tabernacle's light; its branches symbolize peace, mercy, and the covenant community.
Genesis 8:11 WOL ↗
The dove came to him toward evening, and look! there was a freshly plucked olive leaf in its bill. So Noah knew that the waters had receded from the earth.
The olive leaf is the first natural object to appear after the Flood, making it one of Scripture's most iconic symbols of renewal. The olive's ability to survive in harsh, rocky soil — even near saltwater — made this detail vivid to ancient readers. The dove and olive branch became humanity's universal emblem of peace and new beginning.
Exodus 27:20 WOL ↗
You are to command the Israelites to bring you pure, beaten olive oil for the light, to keep the lamps burning regularly.
Olive oil fueled the menorah — the literal light of God's dwelling. Only first-pressed, pure oil was acceptable. The olive's connection to divine light echoes all the way to the two olive trees of Revelation 11, which represent the two witnesses who "stand before the Lord of the earth."
Psalm 52:8 WOL ↗
But I am like a thriving olive tree in the house of God; I trust in God's loyal love forever and ever.
David's image of a flourishing olive tree "in the house of God" is striking — the temple courts did not literally have olive trees. The olive's evergreen nature (it never fully sheds its leaves) reinforces the image of enduring, rooted life in God's presence, contrasted with the wicked who trust in wealth and are uprooted.
Romans 11:17–24 WOL ↗
However, if some of the branches were broken off and you, although being a wild olive, were grafted in among them and became a sharer of the richness of the olive's root, do not be arrogant toward the branches.
Paul's extended olive tree allegory is perhaps the most theologically sophisticated tree metaphor in the New Testament. Israel is the cultivated olive; Gentile believers are wild shoots grafted in. The image teaches both humility (Gentiles are guests, not owners) and hope (natural branches can be regrafted). The WOL has rich commentary on this passage.

05
Cedar of Lebanon
Hebrew: אֶרֶז (Erez) · Greek: κέδρος (kedros)
The most prestigious tree of the ancient world — 100 feet tall, fragrant, nearly incorruptible. Used to build David's palace and Solomon's Temple. A symbol of both divine grandeur and human pride that will be brought low.
1 Kings 5:6 WOL ↗
Now command that cedars of Lebanon be cut for me, and my servants will work alongside your servants, and I will give you the wages of your servants, whatever you say, for you well know that there is no one among us who knows how to cut trees like the Sidonians.
Solomon's request for cedar to build the Temple reveals the tree's sacred significance. The greatest building in Israel's history — the dwelling of God's name — would be built of cedar. Its incorruptibility, beauty, and fragrance made it uniquely fitting for a holy space. Hiram's partnership with Solomon was both political and providential.
Psalm 92:12 WOL ↗
The righteous one will flourish like a palm tree; he will grow tall like a cedar in Lebanon.
The Psalmist pairs the palm (fruitful, productive) with the cedar (majestic, enduring) to capture both dimensions of godly character: active fruitfulness and steady permanence. The cedar's slow growth — centuries to mature — implies that righteous character is not quickly developed but deeply rooted.
Isaiah 2:13 WOL ↗
Against all the cedars of Lebanon that are lofty and tall, and against all the massive trees of Bashan.
Isaiah lists the cedars among the proud things that will be brought low on the Day of Jehovah. The tree's very greatness makes it a candidate for divine humbling. Height without holiness is precarious — a theme that runs from Ezekiel's allegory of Assyria as a cedar (Ezekiel 31) to the ultimate leveling of all human pride.
Isaiah 61:3 WOL ↗
They will be called big trees of righteousness, the planting of Jehovah, for his own glory.
The same Messianic passage Jesus read in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4) promises the redeemed will be called "big trees of righteousness." The cedar image is fully redeemed: from sites of grandeur and pride to a descriptor of God's restored people — strong, enduring, deeply rooted in grace.

06
Palm Tree
Hebrew: תָּמָר (Tamar) · Greek: φοῖνιξ (phoinix)
From the oasis at Elim to the palms of the New Jerusalem — the date palm symbolizes righteousness, victory, and eschatological joy. Jericho was called "the City of Palms," and its carved image adorned Solomon's Temple throughout.
Exodus 15:27 WOL ↗
Then they came to Elim, where there were 12 springs of water and 70 palm trees, and they camped there by the water.
After the bitterness of Marah, the twelve springs and seventy palms of Elim picture abundant divine refreshment. The numbers are significant: twelve springs for Israel's twelve tribes; seventy palms recalling the seventy elders or nations. The palm's shade, fruit, and water associations made it the ideal oasis tree for a people wandering in the desert.
Psalm 92:12 WOL ↗
The righteous one will flourish like a palm tree; he will grow tall like a cedar in Lebanon.
Unlike the cedar (which bears no edible fruit), the date palm is supremely productive: hundreds of pounds of dates annually, fronds for baskets and roofing, trunk for building. The righteous person is not merely impressive — they are life-giving and useful to their community. Both dimensions of godly character appear in this single verse.
John 12:13 WOL ↗
They took branches of palm trees and went out to meet him, and they began to shout: "Save, we pray! Blessed is the one who comes in Jehovah's name, the King of Israel!"
Palm branches were a recognized symbol of Jewish victory and nationalism. The crowd's gesture is politically charged — hailing Jesus as a conquering king. But Jesus riding a donkey, not a war horse, reframes the scene entirely: his kingdom comes through humility. The same palms that welcomed a would-be political king would wave before the Lamb in Revelation 7:9.
Revelation 7:9 WOL ↗
After this I saw, and look! a great crowd, which no man was able to number, out of all nations and tribes and peoples and tongues, standing before the throne and before the Lamb, dressed in white robes; and there were palm branches in their hands.
In Jewish tradition, palm branches were waved at the Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot), celebrating God's provision and pointing to eschatological joy. The redeemed multitude's palms echo both the Triumphal Entry and the ancient harvest festival — celebrating the final, ultimate harvest of souls from every nation.

07
Oak & Terebinth
Hebrew: אַלּוֹן (Allon) / אֵלָה (Elah)
Sacred meeting places throughout Israel's history — where Abraham received God's covenant, Jacob buried foreign gods, Gideon was called, and Joshua sealed the covenant with all Israel. The oak is both a site of divine encounter and, in the prophets, a warning against idolatry.
Genesis 12:6 WOL ↗
Abram passed through the land as far as the site of Shechem, near the big tree of Moreh. At that time the Canaanites were in the land.
The "big tree of Moreh" — likely a terebinth or oak — marks the first sacred site in the Promised Land where God appears to Abram. The name Moreh means "teacher" or "oracle." God meets Abram in Canaan's own sacred landscape, claiming it for his own purposes. The land belongs to Jehovah, not to its current occupants.
Genesis 18:1 WOL ↗
Afterward, Jehovah appeared to him among the big trees of Mamre while he was sitting at the entrance of the tent during the hottest part of the day.
The oaks of Mamre are the setting of one of Scripture's most intimate divine encounters — three visitors share a meal with Abraham, one of whom is Jehovah. Under the ancient, shading oaks, Isaac's birth is announced and Abraham's great intercession for Sodom takes place. Sacred space is simply wherever God chooses to appear.
Isaiah 61:3 WOL ↗
They will be called big trees of righteousness, the planting of Jehovah, for his own glory.
From sites of pagan Canaanite worship to the defining name of God's restored people — the oak image is fully redeemed in this Messianic passage. Strength, endurance, and deep roots — the oak's natural qualities — become the hallmarks of those transformed by divine grace. The prophets reclaim Israel's sacred trees for the kingdom.

08
Almond Tree
Hebrew: שָׁקֵד (Shaqed) — "the Watchful One"
The "awakening tree" — first to bloom after winter, its Hebrew name shaqed (almond) sounds like shoqed (watching). God uses this wordplay to tell Jeremiah: "I am watching over my word to carry it out." Aaron's dead staff sprouted, blossomed, and produced almonds overnight.
Numbers 17:8 WOL ↗
When Moses went into the tent of the Covenant the next day, he saw that Aaron's staff, representing the house of Levi, had sprouted. It had produced buds, blossoms, and ripe almonds.
A dry, dead staff overnight completes a full almond cycle: budding, blossoming, and bearing fruit. The miracle is total — not just life from death but fruitfulness from barrenness. The almond's early-blooming nature ("the wakeful tree") makes it the fitting instrument for God's confirmation of the Levitical priesthood. Aaron's staff was kept in the Ark as a perpetual testimony.
Jeremiah 1:11–12 WOL ↗
The word of Jehovah came to me, saying: "What do you see, Jeremiah?" So I said: "I see a staff of an almond tree." Then Jehovah said to me: "You have seen correctly, for I am watching over my word to carry it out."
One of Scripture's most elegant wordplays — shaqed (almond) and shoqed (watching/wakeful) sound nearly identical in Hebrew. God is the ultimate Watchful One. Jeremiah's very first vision sets the tone for his entire ministry: Jehovah is awake, attentive, and certain to fulfill every word he speaks, however long it takes. The WOL library has detailed notes on this Hebrew wordplay.
Ecclesiastes 12:5 WOL ↗
Also, they are afraid of a high place, and there are terrors along the road. And the almond tree blossoms, the grasshopper drags itself along, and the caper berry is without effect.
In this famous allegory of aging, the almond blossom — white, appearing in winter before all other trees — metaphorically pictures white hair. The Preacher's description of old age is one of Scripture's most extended and tender natural metaphors. The almond's early bloom, once a sign of hope and watchfulness, now marks life's winter arriving.

09
Tamarisk, Broom & Acacia
Hebrew: אֶשֶׁל (Tamarisk) · רֹתֶם (Broom) · שִׁטָּה (Acacia)
Three desert trees that shaped pivotal moments in Scripture: Abraham planted a tamarisk as an act of faith; Elijah collapsed under a broom tree in despair; and acacia (shittim) wood became the sacred material of the Ark of the Covenant and the entire Tabernacle.
Genesis 21:33 WOL ↗
After that Abraham planted a tamarisk tree at Beer-sheba, and there he called on the name of Jehovah, the Everlasting God.
A tamarisk grows slowly and lives for centuries, long outlasting its planter. Abraham's act of planting one was an act of faith — claiming the land, marking a place of worship, and investing in a future he would not personally inhabit. This tree, planted alongside the name "the Everlasting God," speaks to covenant permanence across generations.
1 Kings 19:4–5 WOL ↗
He went a day's journey into the wilderness and sat down under a broom tree, and he asked that he might die. "It is enough! Now, O Jehovah, take away my life, for I am no better than my forefathers." He then lay down and fell asleep under the broom tree.
The broom tree is the setting of Elijah's lowest moment — a prophet who performed the greatest miracle of his career (fire at Carmel) now suicidal with exhaustion and fear. God's response is not rebuke: it is food, rest, and a gentle question. The broom tree (a scraggly desert shrub) mirrors his desolation. Care precedes correction; God ministers to the body before addressing the spirit.
Exodus 25:10 WOL ↗
They are to make an ark of acacia wood, two and a half cubits long, one and a half cubits wide, and one and a half cubits tall.
Acacia wood is the material of the Ark of the Covenant — Israel's most sacred object. Its extraordinary durability (acacia is nearly impervious to insects and rot) and availability in the Sinai desert made it ideal. The Tabernacle's frames, the altars, the table, the lampstand frame — all acacia. The desert scrub tree becomes the foundation of Israel's entire worship system.

12
Mustard Tree
Greek: σίναπι (sinapi)
The Kingdom of Heaven in seed form — starting from the smallest of seeds and growing into something that shelters all nations. Jesus' most famous tree parable, drawing on prophetic images from Ezekiel 17 and 31, claims cosmic significance for the humblest of beginnings.
Matthew 13:31–32 WOL ↗
He presented another illustration to them, saying: "The Kingdom of the heavens is like a mustard grain that a man took and planted in his field. It is, in fact, the tiniest of all seeds, but when it has grown, it is the largest of all vegetable plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds of heaven come and find lodging among its branches."
The contrast between seed (tiny) and tree (enormous) is the entire point: God's kingdom enters history in the most unimpressive form — a Galilean peasant in an occupied land — and grows into something sheltering all nations. The "birds lodging in branches" echoes Ezekiel's great-tree imagery for empires, claiming that same cosmic significance for the humble kingdom of Jehovah.

13
Sycamore-Fig Tree
Greek: συκομορέα (sykomorea)
The tree Zacchaeus climbed to see Jesus — the one moment in the Gospels where a wealthy, dignified man runs and climbs a tree, discarding his status entirely. The sycamore-fig's low, spreading branches made it easy to climb; and Jesus chose that moment to call Zacchaeus by name.
Luke 19:1–4 WOL ↗
He entered Jericho and was passing through. Now a man named Zacchaeus was there; he was a chief tax collector, and he was rich. He was trying to see who Jesus was, but he could not get a view because of the crowd, since he was short. So he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore tree to see Jesus, for he was about to pass that way.
Zacchaeus' act — a wealthy, dignified chief tax collector running and climbing a tree — is socially undignified, reflecting a desperation to see Jesus that transcends status. Jesus' response ("Zacchaeus, hurry and get down, for today I must stay at your house") shows that it is God who seeks us, not merely we who seek God. The tree becomes the hinge of Zacchaeus' complete transformation.

14
The True Vine
Greek: ἄμπελος (ampelos) — "I am the true vine"
Jesus' final "I AM" declaration before his death — the most extended tree metaphor in the New Testament. Israel was called God's vine (Isaiah 5), but failed. Jesus presents himself as the vine Israel was always meant to be. Abiding in him is the key posture of the disciple.
John 15:1–5 WOL ↗
"I am the true vine, and my Father is the cultivator. Every branch in me not bearing fruit, he takes away, and every one bearing fruit, he cleans so that it may bear more fruit... I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever remains in union with me and I in union with him, this one bears much fruit; for apart from me you can do nothing at all."
Israel was repeatedly called Jehovah's vine (Psalm 80:8–16; Isaiah 5:1–7; Jeremiah 2:21), but failed to bear the expected fruit. Jesus presents himself as the True Vine — the one in whom the promise is finally and fully realized. The disciples are branches: dependent, fruitful when connected, useless when severed. "Remaining" (abiding, staying in union) is the key spiritual action.
John 15:6 WOL ↗
If anyone does not remain in union with me, he is thrown out like a branch and dries up. And men gather those branches and throw them into the fire and they are burned.
The vine metaphor carries a solemn warning alongside its promise: a branch has no life independent of the vine. Neither does a disciple have spiritual life independent of Jesus. The image of gathering and burning dry branches echoes John the Baptist's warning (Matthew 3:10) and recalls the broader prophetic picture of unfruitful Israel.

15
The Cross as Tree
Greek: ξύλον (xylon) — "wood / tree / stake"
Peter and Paul deliberately use xylon (tree/wood) rather than stauros (cross) when describing the crucifixion — invoking Deuteronomy 21:23: "Cursed is everyone hung on a tree." What the first tree in Eden inaugurated is reversed by the tree of Calvary.
Galatians 3:13 WOL ↗
Christ purchased us, releasing us from the curse of the Law by becoming a curse instead of us, because it is written: "Accursed is every man hung upon a stake."
Paul quotes Deuteronomy 21:23 directly to explain the atonement. The cross/tree is where the Deuteronomic curse lands — not on the sinner, but on Christ in our place. This is the doctrine of substitutionary atonement explained through tree imagery. The tree that brought the curse becomes the means of redemption from it.
1 Peter 2:24 WOL ↗
He himself bore our sins in his own body on the stake, so that we might die to sins and live to righteousness. And "by his wounds you were healed."
Peter's use of xylon (tree/stake) alongside Isaiah 53:5 creates a profound synthesis. The cross is the tree that bears our sin and produces our healing — resonating with the healing leaves of the Tree of Life in Revelation 22:2. The biblical arc from Eden's tree to the New Jerusalem's tree is one great story, held together by the tree of the cross at its center.
